The Story
Read carefully. Notice the bold words.
🗺️

As the centuries passed, Phoenician ships sailed farther and farther from home. They needed safe outposts — places to take on water, repair ships, and store goods. So they did something remarkable: they founded colonies.

A Phoenician colony was not an empire built by conquest. It was a trading settlement — a community of Phoenician merchants and craftspeople who settled in a new land, built a harbour city, and wove that city into the great Phoenician trading network. These colonies spread across the entire Mediterranean: across North Africa, on the islands of Sardinia, Malta, and Sicily, along the coast of Spain and Portugal, even as far as the Atlantic coast of Morocco.

The greatest of all Phoenician colonies was Carthage — founded by the city of Tyre on the northern coast of Africa, in what is now Tunisia. Carthage grew so powerful that it would one day challenge the might of Rome itself.

But perhaps the most extraordinary gift the Phoenicians gave the world was not their glass, not their purple dye, not even their colonies. It was their alphabet. The Phoenicians developed a writing system of just 22 letters — all consonants, no vowels. It was simple. It was logical. It could be learned in days rather than the years required to learn Egyptian hieroglyphs or Sumerian cuneiform.

The Greeks encountered the Phoenician alphabet through trade. They borrowed it — and added vowels. The Romans borrowed it from the Greeks. The Romans spread it across Europe. Today, when you write a single letter of the English alphabet, you are using a system whose ancestor was first carved as a Phoenician inscription on a stone in Byblos.

The legacy of the Phoenicians lives on in every word you read.

Language Focus
Grammar from the story — learn through meaning
📌 Passive Voice — was / were + past participle
History often uses the passive voice because we focus on what happened, not who did it.
Carthage was founded by the city of Tyre.
The alphabet was borrowed by the Greeks.
Vowels were added to the Phoenician system by the Greeks.
The writing system was spread across Europe by the Romans.
📌 The present perfect — 'has/have + past participle'
Use the present perfect to talk about past events that affect the present.
The Phoenician alphabet has shaped every modern writing system.
Carthage has inspired historians and writers for two thousand years.
The Phoenicians have left a legacy that still exists today.
Trade routes have connected civilisations since ancient times.
📌 Relative clauses — 'whose / which / that'
Use relative clauses to add information about a noun.
The Phoenicians built colonies that stretched across the Mediterranean.
Byblos was a city whose name gave us the word Bible.
Carthage was a colony which grew more powerful than its founding city.
The alphabet is a system whose ancestor was carved in Byblos.

Ideas & Society
Think about big ideas. There is no wrong answer.
🔤
The alphabet connects you to ancient Phoenicia. Every time you write in English, you are using a system first invented on the Phoenician coast. Does this change how you feel about writing?
🌍
Colonisation through trade vs conquest. The Phoenicians built colonies as trading partners, not as rulers over conquered peoples. Is this type of colonisation better? What problems might it still cause?
📚
Writing preserves civilisation. Because the Phoenicians invented a simple alphabet, ideas could be written down, traded, and remembered across generations. What would be lost without writing?
🏛️
Forgotten civilisations, lasting legacies. Most people have never heard of the Phoenicians. Yet their alphabet is used by billions. Why is it important to study civilisations that left no famous monuments?

💡 Big Idea

The Phoenicians built no pyramids and left no famous temples. But they gave the world something more powerful than stone: the alphabet — the tool with which every story, every law, every poem, and every textbook since has been written.
