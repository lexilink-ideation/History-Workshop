2 — The Story
Read carefully. Notice the bold words.
🗺️

The Phoenicians did not just sail the Mediterranean — they claimed it. Wherever their ships found a good harbour, a safe bay, or a source of valuable materials, they stopped. They built. And they stayed.

These settlements were called colonies. A Phoenician colony was a miniature version of home — a small community of Phoenician traders and families, living far from Phoenicia but keeping Phoenician customs, language, and religion. At first, each colony was just a simple outpost: a few warehouses, a dock, and some tents. But the best ones grew into real cities — with walls, temples, and thousands of people.

⚓

The most important Phoenician city at home was Tyre, right on the Canaan coast. Tyre became one of the most powerful trading ports in the ancient world. Ships from Egypt, Greece, Mesopotamia, and beyond arrived and departed every day. The docks were always busy. The markets always loud. The smell of the dye factories always present.

But the Phoenicians expanded far beyond Tyre. They founded colonies across the Mediterranean — in Cyprus, Sicily, Sardinia, Spain, and North Africa. They crossed the Strait of Gibraltar (which the ancients called the Pillars of Hercules) and sailed into the Atlantic. Some historians believe Phoenician sailors may have reached the coast of Britain in search of tin, and possibly even explored the west coast of Africa.

📜

But perhaps the greatest gift the Phoenicians gave to the world was not glass, not purple dye, not cedar wood — it was their alphabet. The Phoenician alphabet was one of the first writing systems in which each symbol represented a sound (a phoneme), not a whole word or idea. It had only 22 letters, all consonants — but it was simple enough that ordinary merchants could learn it quickly to keep their trade records.

The Greeks adopted the Phoenician alphabet and added vowels. They passed it to the Romans. The Romans gave it to Europe. Today, you are reading this page in a descendant of the Phoenician alphabet. When you write the letter A, you are writing a version of a letter that a Phoenician merchant used to record a trade deal 3,000 years ago.

The Phoenicians built a great trading network that spread goods, ideas, and civilisation across the ancient world. Their influence was everywhere — and their alphabet is still with us today.

4 — Language Focus
Grammar from the story
📌 GRAMMAR
Past Simple vs Past Continuous

Use the past simple for completed events. Use the past continuous (was/were + -ing) for ongoing background situations.

Past Simple (completed event)Past Continuous (background situation)
The Greeks adopted the alphabet.The docks were always busy.
The Romans gave it to Europe.Ships were arriving every day.
The Phoenicians founded Carthage.Merchants were trading all around the sea.
When both appear together: use past continuous for background, past simple for the event that interrupted it.
📌 GRAMMAR
Relative Clauses — "which" and "who"

Use who for people and which for things to add extra information.

The Phoenicians, who were the greatest sailors, founded colonies everywhere.
The alphabet, which had only 22 letters, was easy to learn.
Tyre was a city which became one of the most powerful ports in history.
📌 GRAMMAR
Linkers — "not only… but also…"
The Phoenicians were not only traders, but also founders of cities.
Their gift was not only glass and purple dye, but also the alphabet.
This structure is excellent for IELTS writing. It shows two aspects of the same idea with emphasis.

8 — Ideas & Society
Think about big ideas. There is no wrong answer.
✍️

The power of writing. Before alphabets, only trained scribes could read and write. The Phoenician alphabet was simple enough for ordinary merchants. Today, literacy is considered a basic human right. Do you think the ability to read and write is truly the foundation of all other freedoms? Why?

🌍

Cultural spread. The Phoenicians brought their language, religion, art, and ideas to every colony they founded. Is cultural spread always a good thing? Can you think of examples from history where the spread of one culture helped or harmed other cultures?

🏙️

Cities as civilisation. The Phoenicians founded cities wherever they went. Why do you think cities are so important for the development of civilisation? What can cities do that small villages cannot?

🤲

Legacy and memory. The Phoenicians have been gone for 2,000 years. Yet every time you read a book, type a text message, or write your name, you are using their alphabet. What do you hope YOUR legacy will be? What do you want to be remembered for?
