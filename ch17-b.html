2
The Story
Read carefully. Notice the bold words.
📜

Nebuchadnezzar was, without question, one of the most successful rulers in the ancient world. He had rebuilt Babylon from the ruins of war. He had built towering walls and a gate so beautiful that people spoke of it for centuries. His empire was mighty, stretching across a huge area of the ancient Middle East. People began calling him “Nebuchadnezzar the Great.”

But being called “great” is not the same as being happy. Strange as it sounds, ancient clay tablets and scrolls written during Nebuchadnezzar's reign describe something historians now call “Nebuchadnezzar's madness.” For several years, according to these old records, the powerful king actually seemed to lose his mind.

How could such a successful ruler fall into madness? One famous story, found in the Bible in the Book of Daniel, gives us an answer — and it is a warning about pride.

According to the story, Nebuchadnezzar had become a little too pleased with himself. Every day he looked out at Babylon — its towering walls, its beautiful gate, its temples and palaces — and he grew prouder and prouder. Slowly, an extraordinary idea took root in his mind: perhaps he was not simply a great king. Perhaps he was something more. Perhaps, he began to think, he was actually a god.

To prove it, Nebuchadnezzar ordered his workers to build an enormous golden statue in his own image — almost a hundred feet high, gleaming in the desert sun. Then he commanded every person in his kingdom to bow down before the statue and worship it, exactly as they would worship a god.

Think about that for a moment. Nebuchadnezzar was already the most powerful man in his world. He had armies, gold, a magnificent capital city, and the loyalty of millions of people. And still, it was not enough. He wanted to be worshipped like a god.

This is where the ancient story becomes a warning. In the Book of Daniel, pride like this never goes unpunished for long. A king who claims to be equal to God is about to receive a lesson he will never forget.

What happened next changed Nebuchadnezzar forever — turning the most powerful man in the world into something almost unrecognizable. Read on in the next lesson to discover exactly what happened when the king's pride finally went too far.

Language Focus
Grammar from the story — learn through meaning
📌 Irregular Past Tense — More Storytelling Verbs
We use the past tense to describe how Nebuchadnezzar's pride grew.
BasePastFrom the story
becomebecameHe became known as Nebuchadnezzar the Great.
growgrewEvery day he grew prouder and prouder.
thinkthoughtHe began to think he was a god.
makemadeHe made an enormous golden statue of himself.
telltoldHe told his people to worship it.
📌 'So + adjective + that' — showing extreme results
Use this pattern to show that something was so extreme it caused a result.
The statue was so enormous that it was almost a hundred feet high.
He was so proud that he wanted people to worship him.
The empire was so mighty that no one dared to challenge it.
📌 Modals of speculation — 'might' and 'perhaps'
Use 'might' and 'perhaps' to show that someone is guessing or speculating, not stating a fact.
Perhaps he was something more than a king.
Perhaps, he began to think, he was actually a god.
He might have been the most powerful man alive, but he still wanted more.

Ideas & Society
Think about big ideas. There is no wrong answer.
👑
“Power tends to corrupt” is a famous saying. Does Nebuchadnezzar's story support this idea? Why or why not?
🪙
Nebuchadnezzar had everything — gold, armies, a beautiful capital — yet he still wanted to be worshipped like a god. Why do you think having everything is sometimes not enough for powerful people?
🗣️
No one in the story seems to tell Nebuchadnezzar he is wrong. Why might it be dangerous to disagree with a powerful ruler? What happens to societies when no one can criticize their leaders?
📖
This story has survived for thousands of years as a 'warning about pride.' Why do you think ancient cultures used stories, rather than just rules, to teach important lessons?
