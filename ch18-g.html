The Story
Read carefully. Notice the bold words.
🌋

What happened to the Minoans? It's one of history's great unsolved mysteries. More than two thousand years ago, the Minoan civilization came to a sudden end. Every single Minoan left the island of Crete — but why?

Although no one knows for certain, many historians believe the Minoans fled after a massive volcano erupted on a nearby island called Thera. Thera looked like an ordinary island. People lived there, grew crops in its fields, and raised animals — but hidden beneath the surface, Thera was actually the top of an enormous active volcano rising from the Mediterranean Sea.

😨 One day, the ground of Thera began to shake. Small earthquakes rattled the island again and again. Many people fled at once, grabbing their belongings and rushing to safety. But eventually the earthquakes died down, and those who remained decided to stay. They even began rebuilding the walls that had crumbled.

💨 The volcano, however, was far from finished. Bits of rough volcanic rock called pumice began spraying from its funnel, covering the countryside in gray dust. Smoke started rising up out of the ground itself. Seeing this, the rest of Thera's people finally fled the island — and just in time.

🌋 The volcano exploded with tremendous force, hurling lava and rock across the island. Enormous boulders rained down like bombs onto Thera's villages. Then, suddenly, the volcano collapsed inward on itself. The sea rushed into the massive hole left behind, and the entire island of Thera sank beneath the waves — gone forever.

🌊 Crete itself remained above the water, but it did not escape unharmed. A tidal wave, thrown up by the force of the eruption, crashed onto its shores. Thick clouds of ash, dust, and smoke drifted across the island on the wind, blanketing crops and making it hard for anyone to breathe.

🌾 Historians believe that many Minoans abandoned Crete because the volcano had ruined the air and the land. Those who remained faced starvation, as ash destroyed the crops and food supplies disappeared. Eventually, desperate for food, they were forced to fight their own neighbors just to survive. Minoan cities never regained their former strength or power. In the end, ash and dust helped bring the very first great civilization of Crete to its final, mysterious close.

📖 From bull-jumpers who danced with death for glory, to a navy that ruled the waves, to a king's dark secret in the Labyrinth, to a hero's bittersweet homecoming, and finally to a volcano that erased an entire civilization — the story of ancient Crete shows us that even the mightiest peoples of history can disappear, leaving behind only legends, ruins, and unanswered questions.

Language Focus
Grammar from the story — learn through meaning
📌 Modal verbs of possibility — 'may have', 'believe' for uncertain history
Use modals like 'may have' or phrases like 'historians believe' when a historical explanation is likely but not proven.
The Minoans may have left Crete because of the volcano.
Historians believe the eruption ruined the land and air.
The story may not be completely accurate, but it remains our best explanation.
📌 Cause and effect chains — 'as a result', 'because of this'
Use these linking phrases to connect one event to the next result it caused.
The volcano erupted; as a result, ash covered the crops.
Because of this, food supplies were wiped out.
As a result, many Minoans were forced to leave Crete forever.
📌 Past passive review — 'was/were + past participle' across the chapter
Review the passive voice, used often throughout Chapter 18 to describe historical events.
Crops were destroyed by ash and dust.
The island of Thera was swallowed by the sea.
Fourteen young Athenians were sent to Crete every year.

📖 Chapter 18 Summary

On the island of Crete, in the Mediterranean Sea, a people called the Minoans built one of the ancient world's most extraordinary civilizations. Brave young athletes risked their lives as bull-jumpers, training for years before finally leaping over the horns of a charging bull to honor the Minoan gods — a sport that brought incredible glory, but also frequent death, since few bull-jumpers lived past the age of twenty. Away from the arena, Minoan craftsmen built the greatest ships in the ancient world, allowing their king to create history's very first navy, which drove pirates from the Mediterranean Sea and left the greatest palace of Crete so secure it needed no walls at all. The Minoans also passed down an unforgettable legend about their namesake, King Minos, whose palace hid a monstrous secret: the Minotaur, half man and half bull, trapped within a twisting Labyrinth. Every year, Athens was forced to send fourteen young tribute victims to feed the beast — until the brave prince Theseus volunteered to face it himself. With the secret help of Minos's daughter, Ariadne, and her simple ball of wool, Theseus found his way through the maze, killed the Minotaur, and escaped with his companions. But in the joy of victory, Theseus forgot to change his ship's sail from black to white, and his father, King Aegeus, believing his son dead, threw himself into the sea that has been called the Aegean Sea ever since. Finally, more than two thousand years ago, the Minoan civilization itself vanished — and many historians believe the cause was a catastrophic volcanic eruption on the nearby island of Thera, which sank beneath the sea and left Crete covered in ash, its crops destroyed and its people struggling to survive. From bull-jumping arenas to a hero's Labyrinth to a volcano's fury, Chapter 18 shows that even the most powerful civilizations can rise to greatness — and vanish just as suddenly.
