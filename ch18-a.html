The Story
Read carefully. Notice the bold words.
🌊

We've been learning about people who lived near great rivers — the Egyptians, the people of the Indus Valley, the Chinese along the Yellow River, and the Assyrians and Babylonians of Mesopotamia. But now look over at the Mediterranean Sea, and you'll find something completely different: a people who lived entirely surrounded by water.

These people built their homes on an island called Crete — a long, skinny island shaped a little like a duck flying across the sea. Long, long ago, a tribe called the Minoans settled there, and they entertained themselves in a very unusual way: by leaping over bulls.

🐂 Imagine standing on a hard dirt floor in the middle of a huge arena. Crowds all around you are shouting and cheering. Two other people stand beside you — a boy and a girl. None of you carry any weapons. Your hands are empty, and you wear only a simple, loose garment that lets your arms and legs move freely.

Suddenly, a wooden door swings open. A huge black bull charges into the arena, horns sharp and tipped with gold. He paws the earth, shakes his head, and charges straight toward you.

But you don't run. You wait until he is only inches away — then you grab his horns and push yourself upward. You somersault through the air, do a handstand on the bull's back, and land on your feet behind him. Your teammate catches you. The crowd roars! Then you watch your other teammate vault onto the bull's back too.

⚡ If you had been a child in ancient Crete, this might have been your job. The Minoans trained athletic children to become bull-jumpers from a young age. These children learned the same kind of gymnastics children still practice today — somersaulting, balance exercises, and tumbling. But instead of vaulting over equipment, Minoan students first practiced vaulting over small animals like goats. Only after months of training were they finally allowed to leap over the backs of real bulls.

🏛️ Bull-jumping was not just a game. The Minoans believed their gods sometimes took the form of bulls, so bull-jumping festivals were held to honor them. Bull-jumpers who performed well became famous throughout Crete — but as you'll discover in the next lesson, fame came with a serious price.

Language Focus
Grammar from the story — learn through meaning
📌 The Historical Present — putting yourself in the story
Writers sometimes use present tense verbs, even about the past, to make a scene feel exciting and immediate.
You wait until he's only inches away.
A huge black bull charges out into the arena.
The crowd roars!
📌 'Instead of' + -ing — showing an alternative action
Use 'instead of' followed by a verb + -ing to show what happened in place of something else.
Instead of vaulting over equipment, students vaulted over goats.
Instead of running away, you grab the bull's horns.
Instead of fighting the bull with weapons, bull-jumpers used only their bodies.
📌 'So...that' — describing extreme qualities
Use 'so + adjective + that' to show a result caused by an extreme quality.
The island is so long and skinny that it looks like a duck flying.
The bull was so close that you could feel his breath.
The training was so difficult that only the most athletic children succeeded.

Ideas & Society

Bull-jumping was extremely dangerous, yet it was considered an honor. Why do you think dangerous activities are sometimes seen as honorable in different cultures?
🏝️
The Minoans lived completely surrounded by water. How do you think living on an island shapes the way people live, travel, and think?
👦
Both boys and girls took part in bull-jumping together. What does this tell us about how the Minoans viewed boys and girls in their society?
🙏
The Minoans believed their gods could take the form of bulls. Why do you think ancient people often connected animals with gods and religious beliefs?
