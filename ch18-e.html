2
The Story
Read carefully. Notice the bold words.
🌊

When Theseus and the other thirteen young Athenians arrived on the shore of Crete, they were greeted by the cruel King Minos himself, his beautiful daughter Ariadne walking meekly behind him. "More food for the Minotaur!" Minos laughed. "Tonight, you'll visit the bull-man in the Labyrinth beneath my palace."

The fourteen victims were locked away in the prison cells of Knossos to wait for nightfall. But something unexpected had happened: Ariadne had fallen in love with Theseus the moment she saw him.

🕯️ Just before dark, Ariadne found a torch, a sword, and a ball of wool, and crept secretly out of the palace, down to Theseus's cell. "Theseus," she whispered. "Do you want to kill the Minotaur?"

"Yes!" Theseus answered. "But how can I? He lives at the center of the Labyrinth, and no one who enters that maze has ever found their way out again."

"I've brought you a torch to light your path," Ariadne said, "and a sword to kill the monster. Take this ball of wool and tie the end to the doorframe of the Labyrinth. Then let the ball roll forward in front of you. It will lead you straight to the center, because the center is the lowest point of the whole maze. There, you'll find the Minotaur asleep. Kill it — then follow the thread back out to the door."

🌀 She unlocked his cell and let him out. Theseus did exactly as she said, making his way through the dark, twisting passages, his torch throwing strange shadows on the walls while the ball of wool rolled steadily ahead of him. Suddenly, it stopped. Theseus raised his torch and found himself standing in a huge underground room. It stank of some wild animal, and bones lay scattered across the floor. In the middle of the room, on a golden sofa, lay the Minotaur — half man, half bull — fast asleep.

⚔️ Theseus stepped forward, but the monster woke and leaped up with a terrible roar. The two fought for hours in the darkness, until finally Theseus struck the Minotaur down with his sword. Breathing hard, he followed the wool thread back through the maze until, at last, he saw the door to the outside world ahead of him.

🚢 Ariadne had already released his thirteen friends. Together, the group slipped away to the harbor, boarded their ship, and set sail for Athens — free at last from the terror of the Labyrinth.

Language Focus
Grammar from the story — learn through meaning
📌 Sequencing words — 'then', 'suddenly', 'finally'
Use time-sequence words to help a listener or reader follow the order of events in a story.
Then let the ball roll forward in front of you.
Suddenly, it stopped.
Finally, Theseus struck the Minotaur down with his sword.
📌 Past continuous vs. past simple — background action and main event
Use the past continuous for a longer background action, and the past simple for the main event that happens within it.
While the ball was rolling forward, Theseus followed it through the maze.
The monster was sleeping when Theseus finally found it.
As they were slipping away, the ship waited at the harbor.
📌 Imperatives — giving instructions and commands
Use the base form of the verb to give direct instructions, without a subject.
"Take this ball of wool," Ariadne said.
"Tie it to the doorframe," she told him.
"Kill it, and then follow the string back out."

Ideas & Society
Think about big ideas. There is no wrong answer.
🧵
A simple ball of wool solved an impossible problem. What does this teach us about the value of simple solutions to complicated problems?
💔
Ariadne chose love over loyalty to her father and king. When, if ever, do you think it's right to go against your own family for what you believe is right?
⚔️
Theseus fought the Minotaur alone, in the dark, with no one to help him. What do you think it takes to face a frightening challenge completely alone?
🏛️
The Labyrinth was designed specifically so no one could escape. Why do ancient stories often include impossible-seeming challenges for their heroes to overcome?
