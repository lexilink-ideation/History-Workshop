2 — The Story
Read carefully. Notice the bold words.
👑

The Phoenicians founded many colonies around the Mediterranean. But none became more famous than Carthage, on the coast of North Africa. And the legend of how Carthage was founded is one of the most clever stories of the ancient world — the story of Princess Dido.

Dido was a Phoenician princess, the sister of the king of Tyre. She should have been happy. She lived in a palace. She was married to a very wealthy man. But her brother, the king, was jealous of her husband's riches. He wanted all that money for himself. So one day, the king had Dido's husband arrested — and put to death.

Dido was terrified. She knew that she might be next. So in the middle of the night, she gathered her friends, her servants, and as many of her husband's treasures as she could carry. They crept quietly to the harbour, pushed their boats away from the shore, and sailed away into the darkness. They were refugees — people in exile, fleeing for their lives.

⚓

Dido and her friends sailed for many days. Finally, they spotted land — the coast of North Africa. Dido looked at the harbour, the gentle hills, the position near the sea. "This is it," she said. "We will build our city here."

But when they came ashore, they found that the land was already owned. A local chief met them on the beach. He listened to their request and shook his head. "I will not sell you this land," he said.

Dido did not give up. She was determined. She thought quickly — and then she made a clever offer. "Will you sell me only as much land as can be covered by the skin of a single bull?" she asked. The chief thought about this. He imagined a bull's hide laid out flat on the ground — perhaps enough space for a small house. "Very well," he agreed, smiling. He thought he was getting the better of her.

🐂

But Dido was far cleverer than he knew. She brought out a large bull's hide. Then she took a very sharp knife and began to cut. She cut the hide into hundreds of long, thin strips. Then she laid the strips end to end, in a great circle, around a large hill near the sea.

The chief stared. He had promised "as much land as a bull's hide could cover" — and Dido had shrewdly, legally covered an enormous amount of land. He had been outwitted. He had promised, and he could not take it back. He had to negotiate the deal as agreed.

Dido built a tower on the land and named it "Bull's Hide" in memory of her trick. She and her friends settled around the tower and named their city Carthage. And then something extraordinary happened: prosperity. Ships from all over the world came to trade. The little settlement on the hill grew and grew. It became one of the most powerful cities in the ancient world. Dido's perseverance, cleverness, and determination had built an empire from nothing.

4 — Language Focus
Grammar from the story
📌 GRAMMAR
Past Perfect — "had + past participle"

We use the past perfect when we need to show that one past event happened BEFORE another past event.

The king had killed Dido's husband before she fled the city.
She had gathered her friends before the sun rose.
The chief realised she had outwitted him completely.
Timeline: past perfect (further back) → past simple (more recent). "By the time she finished, she had laid out all the strips."
📌 GRAMMAR
Reported Speech — "said that / told him that"

When we report what someone said, we shift the tense back (present → past, past → past perfect).

Direct SpeechReported Speech
"We will build our city here."She said that they would build their city there.
"I will not sell you this land."He said that he would not sell the land.
"Will you sell me as much land as a bull's hide can cover?"She asked if he would sell her as much land as a bull's hide could cover.
📌 GRAMMAR
Despite / In spite of — Showing contrast
Despite all the difficulties, Dido built a great city.
In spite of being a refugee, she became the founder of Carthage.
Despite the landowner's refusal, she found a way to get the land she needed.
Despite / In spite of + noun phrase or -ing. They cannot be followed by a clause directly.

8 — Ideas & Society
Think about big ideas. There is no wrong answer.
🧠

Intelligence vs force. Dido used her mind — not weapons or money — to win. Throughout history, have clever ideas been more powerful than armies? Think of examples. Can you think of any famous cases where brains defeated brute force?

🚺

Women in ancient history. Dido is one of the few women leaders celebrated in ancient stories. Why do you think women's contributions to history are often less well-known than men's? Is this changing today?

🌱

Starting from nothing. Dido was a refugee who had lost everything — her home, her husband, her country. Yet she built Carthage. Do you think adversity (difficulty and hardship) makes people stronger? Or does it just make things harder?

🤝

Promises and ethics. The local chief made a promise without fully thinking it through. Dido held him to it. Was Dido's trick ethical? Is it acceptable to use a clever interpretation of a promise to get more than the other person intended? Where is the line between cleverness and deception?

10 — Review + Chapter 15 Project
Chapter 15 — The Phoenicians: Complete Summary

📖 Chapter 15 in Five Key Points

The Phoenicians lived in northern Canaan, on a narrow coastal strip. Because the land was rocky and unfertile, they turned to the sea and became the greatest sailors and traders in the ancient world.

They traded widely: cedar wood, salt, fish, embroidered cloth, and tin. They built a vast Mediterranean trading network and became the ancient world's first international merchants.

The Phoenicians were master glassmakers who also invented glassblowing — a revolutionary technique that allowed them to create delicate, beautiful objects unlike anything made before.

They made Tyrian purple dye from murex snails — the rarest, most expensive colour in the world, worn only by kings. And they founded colonies across the Mediterranean, including the great city of Carthage.

Their greatest gift to the world was their alphabet — the ancestor of the Greek, Latin, and modern Western alphabets. Every time you read or write, you use a descendant of the Phoenician alphabet.

⭐ Phoenician⭐ Mediterranean⭐ merchant⭐ cedar⭐ glassblowing⭐ murex⭐ purple⭐ colony⭐ alphabet⭐ Carthage⭐ Dido⭐ perseverance

💡 The Big Idea: The Phoenicians show us that geography is not destiny. Poor land, craggy hills, and isolation could have kept them small and powerless. Instead, they adapted — turned to the sea, mastered trade, invented glassblowing, created purple dye, founded cities, and gave the world its alphabet. The lesson of the Phoenicians: the greatest empires are not always built by armies. Sometimes, they are built by clever merchants with a sharp knife and a long, thin strip of bull's hide.
