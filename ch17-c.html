The Story
Read carefully. Notice the bold words.
🏯

One quiet evening, King Nebuchadnezzar walked out onto the rooftop of his magnificent palace in Babylon. Below him stretched the city he had built — towering walls, golden temples, and the beautiful blue Ishtar Gate glowing in the sunset. He looked out over it all, and pride swelled inside his chest.

“Look at this beautiful city I have built!” he said to himself. “I am the most powerful king in the world. No one is greater than I am — not even God.”

The words had barely left his mouth when a voice suddenly boomed down from heaven. “Nebuchadnezzar!” it thundered. “You have become far too proud. You believe you are greater than God Himself. Now listen to your doom: you will act like an animal and eat grass like a cow, until you finally admit that God is more powerful than you are!”

At that very moment, Nebuchadnezzar's mind broke. He became completely insane. Screaming, he ran from his palace and out into the open fields, abandoning his crown, his throne, and everything he had built. He crawled on his hands and knees like a wild animal. Week after week, his knees grew as tough as hooves, and his fingernails curled and lengthened into claws like a bird's talons. He drank rainwater from puddles, slept under bushes, and woke each morning damp with cold dew. His hair grew long, wild, and shaggy, until he looked more like a hairy goat than a king. And every day, true to the warning, he ate grass like a cow in the field.

From a distance, his frightened people gathered to watch this strange, wild creature that had once been their proud ruler. “What is wrong with the king?” they whispered nervously to one another. “He has completely lost his mind!”

For years, this continued. But finally, one day, Nebuchadnezzar lifted his shaggy head and looked up at the sky. Something inside him had changed. “I am not a god,” he said quietly. “I am only a man. And God is more powerful than I am.”

The instant he spoke those humble words, his sanity returned. He rose to his feet — a man once again, not an animal. He looked down at his own hands and understood, at last, exactly who he was: the king of Babylon, not a god, and not a beast either. He returned to his palace to rule his people once more. But he never again claimed to be anything more than human. Pride, he had learned in the hardest way possible, always comes before a fall.

Language Focus
Grammar from the story — learn through meaning
📌 Direct speech — punctuating quotations
Use quotation marks around a speaker's exact words, with the reporting verb outside them.
“I am the most powerful king in the world,” he said.
“Nebuchadnezzar!” the voice said.
“I am not a god,” he said quietly.
📌 Past continuous + past simple — background action and interruption
Use the past continuous for an action in progress, and the past simple for the event that interrupts it.
He was walking on the roof when a voice came from heaven.
While he was living like an animal, his people whispered about him.
As he was looking at the sky, something inside him changed.
📌 'Until' clauses — showing duration and result
Use 'until' to show how long something continues before it changes.
He lived like a wild animal until he admitted that God was more powerful.
His nails grew until they were as long as a bird's claws.
This continued until, one day, he looked up at the sky.

Ideas & Society
Think about big ideas. There is no wrong answer.
⚡
“Pride comes before a fall” is an old saying. Does this story prove that idea true? Can you think of a modern example of someone whose pride led to their downfall?
🐐
Nebuchadnezzar lost everything — his crown, his palace, even his human dignity — because of one boastful sentence. Do you think words alone can really have such powerful consequences?
🙏
The story says humility (admitting “I am only a man”) cured the king. Why do you think humility is often seen as a powerful, even healing, quality in many cultures and religions?
👥
The people whispered about their king rather than confronting him directly. Why do you think people often gossip about a problem instead of addressing it directly?
