2
The Story
Read carefully. Notice the bold words.
⛏️

Nebuchadnezzar wasted no time turning his impossible idea into reality. If Amytis missed the mountains of Persia, then he would build her a mountain — right in the middle of flat, dusty Babylon.

First, he ordered his workers and slaves to haul enormous slabs of rock into the city from distant quarries. This was grueling, difficult labor; each slab was heavy enough to crush a careless worker, and moving them required teams of laborers, ropes, and enormous wooden rollers. Slowly, slab by slab, the rocks piled up in the center of Babylon, forming the skeleton of an entirely artificial mountain — something no city had ever attempted before.

Once the rock structure stood tall, Nebuchadnezzar's workers covered it with thick layers of soil. Then came the part Amytis would love most: gardeners planted the hillside thickly with trees, flowers, and bushes, many of them brought specially from Persia so that Amytis could look out and see plants that reminded her of home.

But a mountain garden faces one enormous problem in the middle of a hot desert city: water. Real mountains have rain and rivers flowing naturally down their slopes. An artificial mountain has neither. So Nebuchadnezzar's engineers designed something remarkable — a pump powerful enough to lift water from the Euphrates River all the way up to the very top of the garden. Once the water reached the summit, gravity did the rest: it flowed back down through channels and irrigation systems, trickling from level to level, exactly like a real mountain stream. Visitors said you could stand at the bottom of the garden and hear water splashing above you, just as if you stood beside a genuine river in the mountains.

Finally, Nebuchadnezzar ordered paths built winding up and down the artificial mountain, so that people could walk among the trees and flowers at every level, just as they might climb a real hillside back in Persia.

When everything was finally finished, Nebuchadnezzar led Amytis outside to see what he had built for her. “My dear,” he said, “you cannot return to Persia. But I have brought a little piece of Persia to you. Now you can walk in your own hillside garden whenever you wish.”

Amytis's garden quickly became famous as the Hanging Gardens of Babylon. Visitors traveled from distant lands just to admire the impossible mountain rising from the flat desert — proof that even the driest, flattest place on Earth could be transformed, with enough determination, engineering, and love.

Language Focus
Grammar from the story — learn through meaning
📌 Sequencing connectors — describing a process
Use words like 'first,' 'once,' 'then,' and 'finally' to explain the steps of a process in order.
First, he ordered workers to haul rock.
Once the structure stood tall, workers covered it with soil.
Then came the planting of trees and flowers.
Finally, paths were built winding up the mountain.
📌 Passive voice — describing engineering and construction
Use the passive voice to focus on what was built, rather than who built it.
Paths were built winding up the mountain.
The rock was covered with soil.
A pump was designed to lift water from the river.
📌 Purpose clauses — 'so that' and 'to + infinitive'
Use these structures to explain the reason or purpose behind an action.
He built paths so that people could walk among the trees.
He planted Persian flowers so that Amytis would feel at home.
Engineers designed a pump to lift water to the top.

Ideas & Society
Think about big ideas. There is no wrong answer.
⛏️
Building the Hanging Gardens required enormous manpower, likely including enslaved workers. How should we feel about admiring ancient wonders that were often built through forced labor?
💧
Ancient engineers solved the difficult problem of moving water uphill without electricity. What modern engineering problems do you think will impress people thousands of years from now?
🌏
Nebuchadnezzar essentially recreated a piece of a different country inside his own capital. Do you think recreating pieces of 'home' in a new place is a healthy way to cope with homesickness?
🏗️
The Hanging Gardens were built for one person's happiness. Do you think it's a good use of a nation's resources for a ruler to build something purely to please one person they love?
