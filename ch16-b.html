2
The Story
Read carefully. Notice the bold words.
📜

Ashurbanipal, the most powerful king in the world, stood on the walls of his favourite city — Nineveh. He had made it beautiful. He built a magnificent palace with high, cool rooms hung with silk in royal blue, scarlet, and bright yellow. Fifteen great gates, decorated with gold and sculptures of bulls and lions, protected the city. Gardens of strange and beautiful plants — trees and flowers brought from faraway lands — spread through the streets.

But standing there, Ashurbanipal felt a strange sadness. "I have all the power in the world," he thought. "But when I am dead, will anyone remember me? A hundred years from now, how will anyone know of my greatness?"

How many of these tablets are left in the ruins of old cities? If they are not rescued, they will crumble away into dust. Then we'll never know these stories from old times.

That night, his chief scribe brought him something special — a clay tablet found in the ruins of Babylon. It was a tale from the court of Hammurabi, the great Babylonian king. No one had ever read it before. Sitting by lamplight that night, reading the ancient words carved into the clay, Ashurbanipal had a brilliant idea.

He would build the greatest collection of knowledge the world had ever seen — a library. He sent his scribes out across the entire empire to collect every clay tablet they could find. He ordered them to talk to the oldest people and write down the stories they remembered from their grandparents — stories that had never been written down. He commanded priests to record their prayers, astrologers to write down the movements of the stars, doctors to write down their medical knowledge, and historians to record everything they knew.

Room by room, the tablets piled up — thousands of them, full of stories, prayers, science, medicine, history, and law. Ashurbanipal had created the world's first library. His wish came true. Although Assyria's wars destroyed many tablets, some still survive today. We remember him not just as a conqueror — but as the world's first librarian.

Language Focus
Grammar from the story — learn through meaning
📌 Reported Thoughts — "he thought / he wondered"
Use "thought" or "wondered" + "that" or a question word to report what someone was thinking.
Ashurbanipal thought that the city was beautiful, but he wanted more.
He wondered how anyone would remember him after he died.
He asked himself whether the tablets would survive.
The scribe knew that Ashurbanipal loved to read.
📌 Sequence Words — Telling a Story in Order
Use sequence words to show the order of events in a story.
First, Ashurbanipal sent his scribes to find clay tablets across the empire.
Then, he ordered them to collect oral stories from elderly people.
After that, he commanded priests and astrologers to write down their knowledge.
Finally, he had collected thousands of tablets and created the first library.
📌 "although" — Unexpected Contrast
"Although" introduces a surprising or contradictory fact. It is more formal than "but."
Although many tablets were destroyed in Assyria's wars, some still survive today.
Although Ashurbanipal was a ruthless conqueror, he also loved books and learning.
Although Nineveh was beautiful, Ashurbanipal feared it would be forgotten.

8
Ideas & Society
Think about big ideas. There is no wrong answer.
📚
Libraries preserve knowledge. Ashurbanipal feared stories would crumble to dust if not collected. What kinds of knowledge do you think would be lost if libraries did not exist? Think about science, history, or culture.
🌟
The desire for legacy. Ashurbanipal asked: "How will anyone know of my greatness after I am dead?" Why do you think powerful people often worry about being forgotten? What makes a truly lasting legacy?
🗣️
Oral vs. written knowledge. Before Ashurbanipal's library, many stories existed only in people's memories. What is the difference between a story told aloud and one written down? Which do you think is more valuable?
🔬
Knowledge as power. Ashurbanipal collected not just stories but also medicine, astronomy, and law. Why do you think leaders want to control knowledge? Is knowledge a form of power?

💡 Big Idea

The same king who burned cities and scattered salt in fields also created the world's first library. Sometimes the most unexpected people leave the most lasting gifts — and knowledge can outlive even the greatest empires.
