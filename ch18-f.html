The Story
Read carefully. Notice the bold words.
🌅

Ariadne and Theseus, together with the freed Athenian companions, sailed through the night and arrived in the harbor of Athens just as the sun rose over the city.

📜 Before they had sailed from Athens, King Aegeus and Theseus had agreed on a simple plan: a white sail would mean a triumphant return, while a black sail would mean tragedy. It was meant to spare the anxious king weeks of uncertain waiting. But in their haste to escape Crete, Theseus and his companions had forgotten one crucial detail — they never changed the ship's sail from black to white.

😢 As the ship approached, the people of Athens rushed forward to greet it. Some were rejoicing at the sight of their returning children — but others were weeping uncontrollably. "Your Highness," one man said to Theseus, his voice breaking, "your father the king was waiting for you, on top of that far distant cliff. When the sun struck the sails of your ship, and he saw that they were still black, he believed you were dead. So he threw himself off the cliff and into the sea below."

👑 Theseus was crowned king of Athens that very day — but it was a bitter celebration for him. He had defeated the Minotaur and freed his city from its terrible tribute forever, yet his victory had cost him his own father's life, all because of a single sail he had forgotten to change.

⚓ In his grief, Theseus built a monument to his father in the harbor of Athens, so that no one would ever forget King Aegeus. And he named the waters that surrounded his city the Aegean Sea, in loving memory of his father — a name the sea still carries today, thousands of years later.

🕊️ The story of Theseus reminds us that even the greatest victories can carry a painful cost. He had saved fourteen young lives and ended years of suffering for his people — but in the joy and haste of escaping Crete, one small forgotten detail changed the course of his life forever.

Language Focus
Grammar from the story — learn through meaning
📌 Past perfect — describing an earlier past action
Use the past perfect (had + past participle) to show that one past action happened before another past action.
They had forgotten to change the sail before they arrived.
Aegeus and Theseus had agreed on a signal before the ship departed.
Theseus had defeated the Minotaur before he sailed home.
📌 Believe/think + that-clause — reporting beliefs
Use 'believed that' or 'thought that' to report what someone believed, even if it turned out to be false.
Aegeus believed that his son was dead.
The people thought that the ship carried tragic news.
Theseus knew that his victory had come at a terrible price.
📌 'In memory of' / 'in honor of' — dedications
Use these phrases to explain that something was created to remember or respect someone.
Theseus named the sea in memory of his father.
He built a monument in honor of King Aegeus.
The Aegean Sea still carries his father's name in memory of him today.

Ideas & Society
