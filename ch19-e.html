2
The Story
Read carefully. Notice the bold words.
🏺

Barbarian wasn't a nice name! It was an insult. You see, the early Greeks thought of themselves as very civilized people. They lived in nice houses made of stone or wood, with their own kitchens and bathrooms.

Greek women stayed home most of the day, supervising the household slaves who did most of the housework and cooking. Some Greek men worked as storekeepers, farmers, or fishermen.

Others were craftsmen — they spent their time weaving cloth, creating pottery, or making other things for use in everyday life. Greek children went to school, or had private tutors. They lived settled, ordinary lives.

But barbarians didn't have houses of their own or regular jobs. They couldn't read or write. And they spent their time wandering around from country to country, attacking the people who lived there and trying to take over.

The Mycenaean Greeks thought that these wandering barbarians were ignorant, smelly, and uncivilized. They knew that Greek weapons and chariots were the strongest around. They figured that they could protect themselves from any barbarian attack — but as we shall see, they were about to be proven very wrong.

Language Focus
Grammar from the story — learn through meaning
📌 'Used to' / 'would' for past habits
Use 'used to' or 'would' to describe repeated past habits or routines that are no longer true today.
Greek women used to supervise household slaves.
Craftsmen would spend their time weaving cloth or making pottery.
Barbarians used to wander from country to country.
💡 These forms describe repeated actions or states in the past, not single events.
📌 Contrast connectors — 'but', 'while', 'whereas'
Use these words to directly compare two different groups or ideas.
Greeks lived in settled houses, while barbarians wandered from place to place.
Greek children went to school, whereas barbarians couldn't read or write.
Greeks saw themselves as civilized, but they viewed barbarians very differently.
💡 'While' and 'whereas' are more formal ways of showing contrast than 'but.'
📌 Relative clauses with 'who'
Use 'who' to add extra information about a person or group of people.
Household slaves who did the housework were supervised by Greek women.
Barbarians, who couldn't read or write, left us no written records.
Craftsmen, who wove cloth and made pottery, created goods for everyday life.
💡 'Who' always refers to people, connecting two ideas into one sentence.

Ideas & Society
Think about big ideas. There is no wrong answer.
🏺
The Greeks defined 'civilized' as having houses, jobs, schools, and writing. What do you think makes a society 'civilized' today? Is it the same definition?
🧵
Craftsmen who wove cloth and made pottery were valued members of Greek society. How important do you think skilled handmade work is in a modern economy dominated by factories?
😔
The Greeks used the word 'barbarian' as an insult for people who lived differently. Can you think of modern words that are used to unfairly judge people from different cultures?
👩‍👧
Greek women supervised households while enslaved people did much of the physical work. How does learning about slavery in ancient societies change the way you think about historical 'greatness'?
