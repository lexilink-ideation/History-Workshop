The Story
Read carefully. Notice the bold words.
🎉

Bull-jumping was more than a sport to the Minoans — it was sacred. The Minoans believed that their gods sometimes appeared in the shape of bulls, so great festivals were held throughout the year to honor them. At the end of every festival, a bull was sacrificed to the gods as a final act of worship.

📯 On festival days, people traveled from every part of Crete to cheer for their favorite bull-jumpers. The arena filled with noise and color as musicians played, priests offered blessings, and crowds pressed close to the walls to watch. For a talented bull-jumper, festival day was the chance to win real glory.

👑 Successful bull-jumpers were treated like royalty. They were given the finest food and the nicest places to live. Kings and nobles showered them with gifts — gold, jewelry, and beautiful clothes — as rewards for their courage in the arena. To the people of Crete, a champion bull-jumper was almost as respected as a prince or princess.

⚠️ But all that glory came at a terrible risk. Bull-jumping was one of the most dangerous activities in the ancient world. A single mistake — a slow reaction, a badly timed jump — could mean death beneath the bull's horns and hooves. Bull-jumpers were very often killed by the very bulls they were supposed to leap over. In fact, few bull-jumpers ever lived to see their twentieth birthday.

🤔 Why would anyone risk their life for a sport? To the Minoans, the answer was simple. Bull-jumping honored the gods, brought pride to their families, and offered a kind of fame that an ordinary life never could. For the bravest young athletes of Crete, the arena was a stage where courage and death walked side by side — and glory belonged only to those daring enough to face the bull.

Language Focus
Grammar from the story — learn through meaning
📌 Passive voice — describing rituals and ceremonies
Use passive voice when the action itself matters more than who performed it.
Bulls were sacrificed to the gods at the end of the festival.
Bull-jumpers were showered with gifts of gold and jewelry.
Festivals were held throughout the year.
📌 'Few' vs 'a few' — talking about small numbers
'Few' means almost none (negative meaning); 'a few' means some, a small positive number.
Few bull-jumpers lived past the age of twenty. (almost none)
A few bull-jumpers became famous throughout Crete. (some did)
Few people today still practice this dangerous sport.
📌 Adverbs of frequency with passive — 'often' + past passive
Adverbs like 'often' or 'always' usually go between the auxiliary verb and the past participle.
Bull-jumpers were often killed by the bulls they faced.
Champions were always welcomed with great celebration.
The gods were regularly honored with festivals.
