2 — The Story
Read carefully. Notice the bold words.
🐚

Cedar wood, salt fish, embroidered cloth, tin, and glass — the Phoenicians traded all of these. But perhaps nothing made them more famous — or more wealthy — than a colour. One colour, in particular: purple.

Today, purple paint costs almost nothing. You can buy it in any art shop. But in the ancient world, purple was the rarest, most expensive, and most powerful colour in existence. And the Phoenicians were its only source.

The secret was a small sea creature called the murex — a type of snail that lived in the rocky waters along the Phoenician coast. Inside each murex snail was a tiny gland containing a yellowish liquid. When this liquid was exposed to sunlight, it turned an extraordinary shade of deep, rich purple.

🔥

Making purple dye was a horrible process — at least for the nose. The Phoenicians collected the murex snails by the thousands. Then they boiled them — snails, shells, and all — in great vats of salt water and lemon juice for ten days. The smell was indescribable. The stench from the dye factories was so famous that "You stink like a man from Tyre" became a favourite insult throughout the ancient world. The city of Tyre smelled so bad that even the wind avoided it, people joked.

But when the boiling was done and the dye was ready, the result was extraordinary. The Phoenicians dipped fine wool into the purple liquid. When it dried, the wool was a glowing, deep, beautiful purple that would never fade — the colour of sunsets and kings.

👑

Purple dye was incredibly scarce. It took thousands of murex snails to make enough dye for a single cloak. The cost was staggering — sometimes a whole year's wages for one purple garment. That is why purple was called "the colour of kings": only kings, emperors, and the wealthiest nobles could afford it.

Wearing purple was the ultimate signal of status and prestige. In Rome, purple would later become the exclusive colour of the Emperor — no one else was permitted to wear it. In Byzantium, children born to ruling emperors were called Porphyrogenitoi — "born in purple."

And it all started with a smelly little snail, boiled on the coast of Phoenicia.

4 — Language Focus
Grammar from the story
📌 GRAMMAR
So… That — Result Clauses

We use so + adjective/adverb + that to show a result that follows from an extreme quality.

The stench was so bad that even the wind avoided it.
Purple was so expensive that only kings could afford it.
It took so many snails that the process lasted ten days.
This structure is very useful in IELTS essays: "The problem is so serious that governments must act immediately."
📌 GRAMMAR
The Only… — Exclusivity

We use the only to show that something is unique — there is no other like it.

The Phoenicians were the only source of purple dye in the world.
Purple was the only colour that could not be reproduced cheaply.
Tyre was the only city with the knowledge to make this dye.
📌 GRAMMAR
Even — Adding Emphasis
Purple was so expensive that even rich nobles struggled to buy it.
The dye was even worth more than silver.
Even the wind seemed to avoid the city of Tyre.
"Even" emphasises that something is surprising or extreme — more than you would expect.

8 — Ideas & Society
Think about big ideas. There is no wrong answer.
👑

Power and colour. In many cultures, colours carry meaning — red for luck, white for purity, black for mourning. Why do you think human beings attach so much meaning to colours? Can you think of any modern examples of colour signalling status or power?

🦺

Luxury and inequality. Purple cloth could only be owned by the very rich. Today, luxury goods like designer bags or supercars serve the same purpose — to signal wealth and status. Do you think it is healthy for societies to have status symbols? What are the benefits and risks?

🌊

The hidden value of small things. A tiny snail, boiled in a pot, produced a dye worth more than gold. This shows that value is not always obvious from appearance. Can you think of anything today that seems ordinary but is extremely valuable?

🏭

Working conditions and beautiful products. The workers in Tyre's dye factories lived with a terrible stench all day long. Do you think it is ethical to benefit from products made in harsh conditions? Who is responsible — the producer, the buyer, or the government?

10 — Review
Remember what you learned!
⭐ murex⭐ stench⭐ luxury⭐ afford⭐ status⭐ scarce⭐ prestige⭐ dip

💡 Value comes from scarcity. The tiny, smelly murex snail became the source of the most powerful colour in history. Not because purple was naturally beautiful — many colours are beautiful. But because purple was rare, difficult to make, and controlled by one people: the Phoenicians. This is one of the oldest lessons in economics: scarcity creates value.
