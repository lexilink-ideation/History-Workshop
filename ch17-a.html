2
The Story
Read carefully. Notice the bold words.
🔥

For hundreds of years, the Assyrian Empire had been the most feared power in the ancient world. Its armies had crushed one kingdom after another. But every empire, no matter how strong, eventually falls — and Assyria's turn had come.

After the great king Ashurbanipal died, the Assyrian Empire began to fall apart. Old enemies saw their chance. Among them were the Babylonians, a people who lived to the south, along the Euphrates River. Long ago, Assyria had crushed Babylon and even flooded the city to punish it. Now the Babylonians wanted revenge.

Assyria's armies could no longer hold their empire together. The Babylonians attacked Nineveh, Assyria's greatest and most beautiful city. They broke down its mighty walls and gates. They tore the doors off Ashurbanipal's famous library and smashed hundreds of his precious clay tablets. Fortunately, some of the tablets survived the destruction, which is why we can still read Assyrian stories today. Nineveh, once the pride of an empire, was left in ruins.

With Assyria defeated, the Babylonians settled down to rule their own empire. It was not quite as large as the Assyrian Empire had been — the Babylonians never conquered Egypt — but it was still enormous, and for many years, the Babylonians were the most powerful people on Earth.

Babylon itself, the very city that Assyria had once flooded, rose from the ashes. The great king Nebuchadnezzar, who became king around 605 BC/BCE, spent much of his reign rebuilding Babylon and making it more beautiful than ever before. He ordered his workers to build huge walls all around the city so that no invasion could ever destroy it again.

In one section of the wall, Nebuchadnezzar built a spectacular gate. It was covered in bright blue tiles and decorated with images of yellow and white bulls and dragons. He named the gate after Ishtar, Babylon's chief goddess of love and war. Every year, a magnificent parade of soldiers, priests, and musicians passed beneath the gate in her honor, and news of its beauty spread throughout the empire.

Nebuchadnezzar became so famous that people called him “Nebuchadnezzar the Great.” He had rebuilt an empire from the ruins of war, and he ruled it with tremendous pride. But as you are about to discover, that very pride would soon cause him serious trouble.

Language Focus
Grammar from the story — learn through meaning
📌 Irregular Past Tense — Storytelling in History
We use the past tense to talk about finished actions in history.
BasePastFrom the story
buildbuiltNebuchadnezzar built huge walls around Babylon.
becomebecameHe became known as Nebuchadnezzar the Great.
breakbrokeThe Babylonians broke down the walls of Nineveh.
riseroseBabylon rose from the ruins of war.
teartoreThey tore the doors off the great library.
📌 'After' + clause — showing sequence in history
Use 'after' to show that one event happened before another.
After Ashurbanipal died, the Assyrian Empire fell apart.
After Assyria was defeated, the Babylonians ruled their own empire.
After the city was destroyed, the Babylonians rebuilt it more beautifully than before.
📌 Passive voice — describing historical events
Use the passive voice when the action is more important than who did it.
Nineveh was destroyed by the Babylonians.
The gate was decorated with bulls and dragons.
Babylon was rebuilt by Nebuchadnezzar.
Huge walls were built all around the city.

8
Ideas & Society
Think about big ideas. There is no wrong answer.
🔄
Empires rise and fall. Assyria was once the most powerful empire in the world, yet it collapsed quickly. What do you think makes a powerful empire fall apart?
⚔️
Revenge shaped this story. The Babylonians destroyed Nineveh because Assyria had destroyed Babylon earlier. Do you think revenge ever truly 'settles the score,' or does it just continue a cycle of violence?
🚪
Beautiful architecture was often built to show power. The Ishtar Gate was covered in bright tiles and decorations. Why do you think rulers spend so much money making gates, walls, and palaces beautiful instead of simple?
📜
Some ancient tablets survived, even though the library was destroyed. Why is it important for historians that at least some ancient records survive destruction and war?
