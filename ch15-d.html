2 — The Story
Read carefully. Notice the bold words.
💨

The Phoenicians were already the finest glassmakers in the ancient world — but then they invented something that changed glassmaking forever. Something so clever, so simple, and so beautiful that it seems almost magical: glassblowing.

Before glassblowing, all glass was made by pouring the molten liquid into moulds. The shapes were limited. The glass was thick and heavy. But around 50 BC, a Phoenician glassmaker had an idea.

He picked up a hollow metal pipe. He dipped one end into a pot of hot, sticky, liquid glass. A glob of glass clung to the tip of the pipe. Then — very carefully, very gently — he blew through the other end of the pipe.

🫧

Something extraordinary happened. The glass at the tip of the pipe began to puff out. It expanded. It formed a round bubble — like a soap bubble, but made of glowing hot glass. The glassmaker kept blowing, and the bubble grew larger and larger.

Then, while the glass was still soft and warm, he began to shape it. He could stretch the bubble long and thin to make a bottle. He could pinch it and twist it to make a jug. He could roll it against a flat surface to make a bowl. The glass responded to every movement of his hands like clay, but shimmered with light and colour.

When he was satisfied with the shape, he cooled the glass quickly. It hardened into a vessel — a bottle, a bowl, a jug — that was lighter, thinner, and more delicate than any object ever made from poured glass. The technique required great skill, but the results were extraordinary.

🏺

Blown glass was more beautiful AND more expensive than regular glass. Its walls were thin as paper. Light passed through it in ways that seemed almost alive. A Phoenician blown glass bottle was the most sophisticated object in the ancient world — and people paid fortunes to own one.

The secret of glassblowing spread slowly. But for centuries, the Phoenicians kept their advantage. And the world had never seen anything like it.

4 — Language Focus
Grammar from the story
📌 GRAMMAR
While — Simultaneous Actions

We use while to show that two actions happen at the same time.

While the glass was still soft, the glassmaker shaped it.
He blew through the pipe while slowly rotating it.
While some people traded goods, others perfected glassblowing.
Notice: the clause with "while" often uses the past continuous (was + -ing) to show a background action that was in progress.
📌 GRAMMAR
Comparative Adjectives — More / -er

We use comparatives to say that something has more of a quality than something else.

AdjectiveComparativeFrom the story
beautifulmore beautifulBlown glass was more beautiful than poured glass.
thinthinnerThe walls were thinner than any poured glass.
lightlighterBlown glass was lighter than regular glass.
expensivemore expensiveIt was more expensive than ordinary glass.
Short adjectives (1-2 syllables): add -er. Long adjectives (3+ syllables): use "more".
📌 GRAMMAR
Could — Past Ability (review)
He could stretch the bubble to make a bottle.
He could pinch and twist it into any shape.
The Phoenicians could make objects no one had ever seen before.

8 — Ideas & Society
Think about big ideas. There is no wrong answer.
💡

The accidental inventor. Nobody planned to invent glassblowing — a craftsman simply tried something new. Can you think of other great inventions or discoveries that happened by accident? (Penicillin? The microwave? Post-it notes?) What does this tell us about how innovation works?

🌬️

Breath as a tool. The Phoenician glassblower used his own breath to shape glass. Today, we use machines and robots to make things. Do you think handmade objects have a special value that machine-made objects do not? Why?

💎

Beauty and exclusivity. Phoenician blown glass was so expensive that only the wealthy could afford it. Does making something rare and expensive make it more desirable? Is there anything wrong with luxury goods that most people cannot afford?

🔄

Technology spreads. The Phoenicians tried to keep glassblowing secret, but eventually it spread. Today, technology like AI or satellite navigation is spreading across the world rapidly. Is the spread of technology always a good thing?
