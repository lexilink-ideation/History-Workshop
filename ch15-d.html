2
The Story
Read carefully. Notice the bold words.
💨

For centuries, Phoenician craftsmen made glass the hard way: melting it, pouring it into moulds, shaping it around clay cores. It was slow. It was difficult. Each piece took hours of careful work. And so glass remained extraordinarily rare and expensive.

Then, somewhere around 50 BCE, in the Phoenician city of Sidon, an unknown craftsman changed everything. He dipped a long metal blowpipe into a furnace full of molten glass. He gathered a glowing blob of hot glass on the end of the pipe. He lifted it out, put the other end to his lips — and blew.

The molten glass expanded. It stretched outward in all directions, forming a thin-walled bubble of glass. It was perfectly round. It was hollow. And it had taken just thirty seconds to create. The art of glassblowing had been born.

The craftsman had to work quickly and keep rotating the pipe. If he stopped spinning the pipe, gravity would pull the soft glass downward and ruin the shape. He could squeeze the bubble with wooden tools while it was still soft. He could blow it bigger. He could shape it into a vessel — a bottle, a cup, a jar.

Glassblowing was a revolution. A skilled glassblower could now make in minutes what had previously taken hours. The workshops of Sidon began producing glass in huge quantities. Prices fell. Glass spread across the world. For the first time, ordinary people — not just kings — could own glass vessels.

This technique was one of the most important inventions of the ancient world. It is still used today, almost exactly as the Phoenicians discovered it.

Language Focus
Grammar from the story — learn through meaning
📌 Sequencing a process — while, until, when
Use these time connectives to describe what happens at each stage of a process.
While the glass was still molten, he blew into the pipe.
When he stopped rotating, the glass began to droop.
He kept spinning the pipe until the glass hardened.
As soon as the bubble formed, he began shaping it with wooden tools.
📌 Could / could not — past ability
Use 'could' to talk about what was possible in the past.
A glassblower could create a bottle in just minutes.
Before glassblowing, craftsmen could not make glass quickly.
He could shape the bubble while it was still soft.
Ordinary people could not afford glass until glassblowing was invented.
📌 Cause and result — 'led to' and 'as a result'
These phrases link a cause with its effect.
Glassblowing led to a dramatic fall in glass prices.
As a result, ordinary people could afford glass vessels for the first time.
The new technique resulted in huge quantities of glass being produced.
Prices fell. As a result, glass spread across the entire ancient world.

Ideas & Society
Think about big ideas. There is no wrong answer.
💡
One person's invention can change the whole world. An unknown craftsman in Sidon invented glassblowing — and we still use the same technique today. How do we ensure inventors get credit for their work?
📉
When prices fall, products reach more people. Glass went from luxury item to everyday object. Can you think of other technologies that went through a similar change? What were the social effects?
🤲
Skills passed down through generations shape history. Glassblowing was taught master to apprentice for centuries. Why is it important to preserve traditional crafts even in a modern world?
⚗️
Precision and patience are essential for fine craft. A glassblower has only seconds to shape the glass before it hardens. How do you think craftsmen developed such skill without modern training tools?

💡 Big Idea

A single craftsman in Sidon, with just a metal pipe and a breath of air, changed how the world makes things. Innovation does not always come from great armies or rich governments — sometimes it comes from one pair of skilled hands.
