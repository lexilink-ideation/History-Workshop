The Full Chapter Story
The complete tale of Chapter 16 — read it all the way through
⚔️

Long ago, the Assyrians had been conquered by the Babylonians. But they never forgot their dream of freedom. One day, they rebelled. They dug canals through Babylon and flooded it, washing the city away. Then they rebuilt their power — conquering cities across the land, scattering the Israelites, and even marching all the way to Egypt.

The greatest Assyrian king of all was Ashurbanipal, who became king around 668 BC. He terrified his enemies. For fun, he hunted lions on horseback. His armies fought behind light basket shields, marching like a moving wall of arrows. When cities hid behind their walls, he ordered his men to build dirt ramps and wooden siege towers on wheels, crashing through the walls with battering rams.

We are like an evil rain that washes its enemies away! We are like a net that tangles the feet of those who fight against us!

Ashurbanipal was ruthless — burning, destroying, and scattering salt in fields so crops could not grow. He took people as slaves and sent them away from their homes forever. His empire was the largest the world had ever seen. But all across it, people hated him and prayed for his fall.

Then something unexpected happened. One night, a scribe brought Ashurbanipal an ancient clay tablet from the ruins of Babylon — a tale from Hammurabi's court that no one had ever read. Sitting by lamplight, reading those ancient words, the fierce conqueror had a beautiful idea: he would build a library. He would collect all the knowledge of the world and keep it safe in Nineveh forever.

He sent scribes across his empire to collect every tablet they could find. He ordered them to talk to the elderly and write down stories passed down from grandparents. Priests recorded prayers. Astrologers wrote the movements of the stars. Doctors wrote down their medical knowledge. Historians recorded everything they knew. Room by room, thousands of tablets filled Ashurbanipal's palace — stories, science, medicine, law, and history all in one place.

He had created the world's first library. Assyria's wars would eventually destroy many tablets. But some survived. We still remember Ashurbanipal today — not just as a conqueror, but as the world's first librarian. His army is forgotten. His library lives on.

Grammar Review
Key language from both lessons
📌 Review — Irregular Past Tense
History stories use many irregular past forms. Can you complete these?
Shamshi-Adad built (build) the first Assyrian Empire.
Ashurbanipal led (lead) his soldiers into Egypt.
He became (become) king around 668 BC.
Some clay tablets survived (survive) thousands of years.
📌 Review — "so that" (purpose) and "although" (contrast)
Both connectors appear in the chapter. Choose the right one for each idea.
He scattered salt in the fields so that no crops would grow. (purpose)
Although Ashurbanipal was cruel, he loved books and learning. (contrast)
Cities obeyed him so that he would not destroy them. (purpose)
Although many tablets were destroyed, some survive today. (contrast)
📌 Review — Sequence Words in History Writing
Use "First / Then / After that / Finally" to organise events in clear order.
First, the Assyrians rebelled and flooded Babylon.
Then, they conquered Egypt, Canaan, and Asia Minor.
After that, Ashurbanipal ordered scribes to collect tablets from across the empire.
Finally, he created the world's first library in Nineveh.

Ideas & Society
Connect the whole chapter to the big world — no wrong answers
⚖️
Power vs. knowledge. Ashurbanipal used military power to build his empire, then used the power of knowledge to seek his legacy. Which do you think creates a more lasting impact on history — armies or ideas?
🔥
Destruction and creation. The same king who destroyed Babylon also created the world's first library. History is full of people who both destroy and create. Do you think these two sides can exist in the same person? Does it make them complicated or simply human?
😤
Resistance and hope. Conquered peoples told Ashurbanipal: "When we hear of your fall, we will clap our hands with joy." Even when people have no power, they still hold on to hope. Why do you think hope is so difficult to conquer?
📖
The purpose of history. We know about Ashurbanipal today because of his clay tablets. Why do you think it is important to study history? What can we learn from the story of Assyria's rise and fall?

📖 Chapter 16 Summary

The Assyrians had been conquered by Babylon — but they never gave up. They rebelled, flooded Babylon with water, and began rebuilding their empire. Under their greatest king, Ashurbanipal (668 BC), they conquered Egypt, scattered the Israelites, and built the largest empire the world had ever seen. Ashurbanipal's soldiers fought with clever basket shields and siege towers. He was ruthless — burning cities, salting fields, and taking slaves. Yet he is remembered above all for something extraordinary: reading an ancient clay tablet by lamplight, he was inspired to build the world's first library in Nineveh. He sent scribes across his empire to collect stories, prayers, medicine, and history. Some of those tablets still survive today. His army is forgotten. His library lives on.

💡 The Big Idea of Chapter 16

Ashurbanipal built the most feared empire in the world — and then built something even more powerful: a library. The sword makes enemies; the pen makes history. Knowledge, preserved and shared, outlasts every army and every empire.
