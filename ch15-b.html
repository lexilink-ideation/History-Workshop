2 — The Story
Read carefully. Notice the bold words.
⛵

The Phoenicians were the greatest sailors in the ancient world — but why? Because they were also the greatest traders. Sailing and trading went together perfectly. The more they sailed, the more goods they found. The more goods they found, the more they could trade. And trade made them rich.

So what did the Phoenicians sell? Almost everything. Let's take a walk through a Phoenician marketplace and find out.

First: cedar wood. The tall cedar trees that grew in the mountains behind the Phoenician coast were magnificent — strong, sweet-smelling, and resistant to rot. Everywhere else in the ancient world, wood was scarce. So the Phoenicians cut down the cedars, turned them into logs, and floated them along the coast to countries like Egypt. In Egypt, there were almost no trees. A cargo of cedar was worth more than gold. The Phoenicians built beautiful furniture from cedar and sold it for a very high price. They grew very wealthy from this trade alone.

🐟

Next: salt and dried fish. The Mediterranean Sea was full of fish, and salt — used to preserve food before refrigerators existed — was one of the most important products in the ancient world. The Phoenicians salted and dried their fish and sold it to cities far away from the sea.

They also sold embroidered cloth — beautiful fabric decorated with colourful patterns sewn in thread. Cloth-making was a skilled art in Phoenicia, and their fabrics were famous for their beauty and quality. Women in faraway cities wanted Phoenician cloth for their robes and curtains.

⛏️

The Phoenicians also sailed to distant shores to find metals. They found places to dig up tin — a precious metal used to make bronze. Bronze was harder than copper alone and was used to make weapons and tools. Whoever controlled the tin controlled the armies. The Phoenicians traded tin from as far away as Spain and Britain, connecting the Mediterranean world to the Atlantic coast.

By trading all these goods — wood, salt, fish, cloth, and metals — the Phoenicians built the most powerful trading network in the ancient world. Their ships were everywhere. Their language and their goods spread to every shore of the Mediterranean Sea. Wherever there was demand, the Phoenicians were there to export what people needed — and to take home a handsome profit.

The world had its first great international merchants.

📌 GRAMMAR
The more… the more… — Parallel Comparatives

This structure shows that two things increase together. When one thing gets bigger, the other gets bigger too.

The more they sailed, the more goods they found.
The more goods they found, the more they could trade.
The more they traded, the richer they became.
This is a very useful pattern for IELTS essays: "The more countries invest in education, the more productive their economies become."
📌 GRAMMAR
Used to — Habits and States in the Past

We use used to + verb to describe something that happened regularly in the past but doesn't happen now.

People used to preserve food with salt before refrigerators existed.
Phoenician merchants used to sail from country to country selling their goods.
Notice: "used to" has no present form. We can't say "I use to go." We say "I usually go" for present habits.

8 — Ideas & Society
Think about big ideas. There is no wrong answer.
🌍

Trade connects cultures. The Phoenicians brought their goods — and their language, art, and ideas — to every shore of the Mediterranean. Do you think trade always brings cultures closer together, or can it also cause conflict? Why?

🪵

Natural resources and power. The Phoenicians controlled the cedar forests and could find tin. This gave them enormous power. Today, countries that control oil or rare metals have similar power. Is it fair that geography gives some countries so many resources and others so few?

💰

Profit and ethics. The Phoenicians made huge profits from selling goods that others needed. Do you think businesses today have a responsibility beyond making profit? What responsibilities might they have to society or the environment?

📦

What do we trade today? Think about the goods in your daily life — clothes, electronics, food. Where do they come from? How is global trade today similar to and different from Phoenician trade 3,000 years ago?
