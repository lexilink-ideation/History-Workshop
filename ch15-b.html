The Story
Read carefully. Notice the bold words.
⛵

A Phoenician merchant woke before dawn. His ship was loaded with cargo: tall logs of sweet-smelling cedar timber, bolts of fine linen cloth decorated with embroidered patterns, jars of wine, and barrels of salted fish. The wind was right. It was time to trade.

The Phoenicians did not grow much food. But they had things that other nations desperately wanted — especially cedar. The great forests of Lebanon produced the finest timber in the ancient world. Egypt had almost no trees of its own. So Egypt traded grain and gold in exchange for Phoenician cedar. The Phoenicians used that grain to feed their cities, and that gold to buy more goods.

The Phoenicians were the middlemen of the ancient world. They bought goods where they were plentiful and cheap, then sailed them to places where those same goods were scarce and valuable. This was their genius: turning geography into profit.

Their trading routes stretched across the entire Mediterranean. They sailed west to Spain and north to Britain, where they found tin — a precious metal needed to make bronze. Phoenician ships also carried glass beads, fine cloth, olive oil, and wine.

Back home, Phoenician craftswomen created beautiful goods. They made embroidered cloth decorated with bright colours. They wove fine linen that kings and queens across the world coveted. They pickled fish in salt and packed them in clay jars. Every surplus resource became a profit.

The Phoenicians did not build empires with armies. They built something more lasting — a network of trade that connected the ancient world from one end to the other.

📌 Comparative adjectives — '-er' and 'more'
Use comparative adjectives to compare two things.
Cedar was more valuable in Egypt than in Phoenicia.
Tin was scarcer in Egypt than in Britain.
Phoenician cloth was finer than what most people could make at home.
The longer the route, the higher the profit.
📌 Purpose — 'to' + verb / 'in order to'
Use these structures to explain WHY someone did something.
Phoenicians sailed to Britain to find tin.
They salted fish in order to preserve them for the journey.
They traded cedar to get grain and gold.
They built ships in order to expand their trading routes.

Ideas & Society
Think about big ideas. There is no wrong answer.
💱
Trade connects people who need different things. Egypt had grain but no timber. Phoenicia had timber but needed grain. How does this difference create trade? Can you think of a modern example?
🌍
Phoenician traders created one of the world's first global networks. Long before the internet, the Phoenicians were connecting distant peoples through trade. Do you think ancient trade was a kind of 'globalisation'?
⚖️
The Phoenicians made profit by being in the middle. Is it fair to buy cheaply and sell expensively? When does this become unfair or exploitative?
🚢
Risk and reward: long ocean voyages were extremely dangerous. The Phoenicians risked their lives at sea. Do you think the reward of profit justifies this kind of risk? How do people today take similar risks?
