The Story
Read carefully. Notice the bold words.
🌍

By the time Nebuchadnezzar's engineers finished their work, something extraordinary rose from the flat, dusty land of Babylon: a lush, green mountain, covered in trees and flowers, with cool water trickling down through hidden channels from top to bottom. People came from every corner of the ancient world simply to stand and stare in awe.

They called it the Hanging Gardens of Babylon — even though the garden did not actually 'hang' in the air. Historians believe the name came from the way the trees, vines, and flowers cascaded down the terraces, level after level, almost as if they were hanging from the sky itself.

The Hanging Gardens became so famous, so remarkable, and so incredible that ancient writers eventually placed it on a very special list: the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World. This list included the most astonishing structures ever built by human civilization — monuments so impressive that people believed everyone should see them at least once in their lifetime, if they possibly could.

You have already studied one of the other Seven Wonders in an earlier chapter: the Great Pyramid of Giza, built by the ancient Egyptians as a tomb for their pharaoh. Today, you have learned about the second wonder on that famous list — a garden built not for a dead pharaoh, but for a living, homesick queen.

Make a quick comparison between these two wonders. The Great Pyramid was built from millions of massive stone blocks, designed to last forever as a monument to a powerful ruler. The Hanging Gardens, by contrast, were softer and more delicate — made of soil, water, and living plants, designed simply to bring comfort and joy to one unhappy woman, far from home. Yet both structures required incredible engineering, enormous labor, and remarkable imagination. Both became legendary. And both left a legacy that people still discuss thousands of years later.

Interestingly, unlike the Great Pyramid, no one has ever found solid physical ruins of the Hanging Gardens. Some historians even debate whether the garden truly existed exactly as the ancient stories describe, or whether parts of the tale grew more exaggerated over time as it was passed down. But whether every detail is completely accurate or not, the story endures — a timeless reminder that even the most powerful rulers can be moved by love, and that human ingenuity can create marvels wherever there is enough determination.

From the fall of Nineveh, to the madness of a proud king, to a marriage of convenience, to a mountain built from nothing but love and clever engineering — Chapter 17 has taken you through the incredible rise of the Babylonian Empire under Nebuchadnezzar the Great.

Language Focus
Grammar from the story — learn through meaning
📌 Comparative structures — comparing two wonders
Use comparative forms to highlight differences and similarities between two things.
The Pyramid was more permanent than the Gardens.
The Gardens were softer and more delicate than the solid stone Pyramid.
Both wonders were equally remarkable, even though they were very different.
📌 Present perfect — things that still matter today
Use the present perfect for past actions that connect to the present.
The Hanging Gardens have become one of history's most famous stories.
No one has ever found solid ruins of the garden.
Historians have debated this question for centuries.
📌 Concessive clauses — 'even though' and 'although'
Use these words to connect a surprising or contrasting idea to the main clause.
Even though no ruins have been found, the story endures.
Although the garden may not have existed exactly as described, it remains legendary.
Even though Nebuchadnezzar had once been humbled, he still loved his wife enough to build her a mountain.

📖 Chapter 17 Summary

After the fall of Ashurbanipal, Assyria collapsed and the Babylonians took revenge, destroying Nineveh, before rebuilding their own mighty empire under Nebuchadnezzar the Great, who built towering walls and the beautiful Ishtar Gate. Despite his success, ancient tablets describe “Nebuchadnezzar's madness” — a story from the Book of Daniel in which the proud king claimed to be a god, was struck down by a divine voice, and lived like a wild animal for years until he humbly admitted he was only a man. Later, fearing invasion from the rising power of Persia, Nebuchadnezzar arranged a marriage with the Persian princess Amytis to secure peace between the two empires. Homesick for the mountains of her homeland, Amytis found no comfort in gold, silk, or servants — so Nebuchadnezzar built her an artificial mountain garden in the heart of flat, dry Babylon, complete with a pump lifting water from the Euphrates River. Known as the Hanging Gardens of Babylon, the garden became the second of the Seven Wonders of the Ancient World that you have studied, standing as a timeless monument to one king's pride, humility, fear, and love.
