The Exodus from Egypt

How a band of enslaved people escaped the most powerful empire on Earth — and changed the course of history forever.

🕍
Chapter 1
Moses discovers the truth

Moses grew up in the palace of the pharaoh — but as an adult he discovered he was not Egyptian. He was an Israelite, and he saw his people being beaten and enslaved.

He went before the pharaoh and said: "I am an Israelite, and I worship the one God of the Israelites. God says: Let the Israelites go!" The pharaoh refused to let his slaves go free.

Israelites
The ancient people of Israel; Moses's people who were enslaved in Egypt
Pharaoh
The king of ancient Egypt; one of the most powerful rulers in the ancient world
⚡
Chapter 2
The ten plagues of Egypt

Moses told the pharaoh that God would send ten terrible plagues on Egypt. Each plague was a direct challenge to an Egyptian god — proving the God of Israel was more powerful. The Egyptians believed Horus ruled the Nile, Isis controlled frogs, and Ra commanded the sun.

🩸
Nile to blood
🐸
Frogs everywhere
🌑
Total darkness
🦟
Locusts & flies
💀
Death of firstborn

After ten devastating plagues, the pharaoh finally told Moses the Israelites could leave. They packed all their belongings and left Egypt that very night.

But then the pharaoh changed his mind and sent his whole army after them.

Plagues
Terrible disasters sent as punishment; each one affected all the people of Egypt
Monotheism vs polytheism
Mono = one god (Israelites). Poly = many gods (Egyptians: Horus, Ra, Isis...)
🌊
Chapter 3
Trapped at the Red Sea

The Israelites ran as fast as they could, but the Egyptian army was close behind. Ahead of them lay the Red Sea — water in front, and soldiers behind. They were completely trapped.

Then Moses raised his staff. God parted the waters — huge walls rose on either side. The ground beneath was dry. They could even see fish swimming in the walls of water as they walked through to the other side.

The Egyptians drove their war chariots into the parted sea. Moses raised his staff again. The water flooded back and drowned the entire army. The Israelites were finally free.

Parted
Split apart; divided into two halves to create a dry path through the middle
War chariots
Fast horse-drawn vehicles used by soldiers in ancient battles
🌿
Chapter 4
The Exodus — freedom and its meaning

The Israelites walked from Egypt all the way to Canaan — the land where Abraham had once lived. They lived there for many years and became a powerful kingdom in their own right.

This event is now called the Exodus. It shows monotheism winning out over polytheism: the one God of Israel conquered the many gods of Egypt. The Israelites were free.

The story also tells us something important about Egypt. Even a band of unarmed slaves could escape Egypt's mighty army. The New Kingdom of Egypt, which had once come close to ruling the world, was losing its strength and beginning to decline.

The Exodus
The Israelites' great escape from slavery in Egypt; one of the most important events in ancient history
Canaan
The ancient land promised to Abraham's people; modern-day Israel and Palestine

Activity 1 — Vocabulary flash cards Tap to reveal

Tap each card to see the definition. Try to guess it first!

enslaved
adjective
Forced to work for someone else with no freedom or pay; treated as property
plague
noun
A terrible event or disease causing great suffering; God sent 10 plagues on Egypt
monotheism
noun
The belief in only ONE god. From Greek: mono (one) + theos (god). The Israelites were monotheists
polytheism
noun
The belief in MANY gods. The Egyptians worshipped Horus, Ra, Isis, and many others
exodus
noun
A mass departure of a large group of people. The Israelites' escape from Egypt is called "the Exodus"
decline
noun / verb
To become weaker or less powerful over time. After the Exodus, Egypt began to decline
parted
verb (past tense)
Split apart; divided into two sections. God parted the Red Sea so the Israelites could cross
conquer
verb
To defeat and take control over something or someone. The God of Israel conquered the gods of Egypt

Activity 5 — IELTS speaking practice Speaking

Use these prompts to practise your speaking skills. Aim to speak without stopping for the suggested time.

Part 1 — short answers
Do you enjoy learning about ancient history? Why or why not?

Do you know any stories from your own culture about freedom or escape?

Have you ever visited a museum or historical site? Describe it.
Speak for 20–30 seconds per question
Part 2 — long turn (cue card)
Describe a historical event you find interesting.

You should say:
• what happened
• when and where it took place
• why it is remembered
• and explain how it changed the world
Speak for 1–2 minutes
Part 3 — discussion
Why do you think stories like the Exodus are still told today?

Is religion a powerful force in shaping history? Give examples.

What can ancient history teach modern societies about power and freedom?
Discuss and give reasons for your views
Useful language for speaking
Giving opinions: I believe... / In my view... / It seems to me that...
Giving examples: For instance... / A good example is... / As we can see from...
Contrast: However... / On the other hand... / Although...
Adding ideas: Furthermore... / In addition... / Not only that, but...
